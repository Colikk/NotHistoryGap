Plot Summary

Nuremberg follows the International Military Tribunal convened in Nuremberg, Germany from November 1945 to October 1946 — the first and most significant of the post-World War II war crimes trials. The film centers on U.S. Chief Counsel Robert H. Jackson (Alec Baldwin), a sitting Supreme Court Justice who leads the American prosecution team against 21 surviving senior Nazi leaders.


The narrative unfolds as a legal drama and psychological character study, contrasting Jackson's idealistic mission to establish international law with the cold defiance of Hermann Göring (Brian Cox), Hitler's second-in-command, who attempts to turn the proceedings into a platform for Nazi ideology. The film covers the formation of the four-power tribunal (U.S., UK, France, and USSR), the presentation of devastating documentary evidence including footage from liberated concentration camps, the individual testimonies and defenses of each defendant, and the final verdicts — including death sentences by hanging for twelve of the accused.

Characters

Historic
Robert H. Jackson
Principled, idealistic — a justice on a mission to build something that outlasts the verdict
Historic

Jackson was the real U.S. Chief Counsel and a sitting Associate Justice of the Supreme Court. The film accurately captures his belief that the trials needed to be more than victor's justice — he wanted to establish a precedent for international law. His contentious cross-examination by Göring, which Göring "won" in many observers' eyes, is one of the most accurately portrayed moments in the film. Alec Baldwin plays him as earnest and sometimes outmatched, which lines up with historical accounts.

Hermann Göring
Cunning, theatrical, utterly unrepentant — and the prosecution's greatest challenge
Historic

Brian Cox delivers one of the most historically grounded portrayals in the film. Göring really was the dominant personality in the dock — alert, intelligent, and determined to use the proceedings for propaganda. He genuinely outmaneuvered Jackson during cross-examination, forcing the tribunal to impose stricter yes/no rules afterward. The film accurately depicts his mood swings, his detox from paracodeine addiction during the trial, and his eventual suicide by cyanide capsule the night before his scheduled execution.

Rudolf Hess
Hitler's former Deputy, feigning amnesia — or not feigning it
Historic

The film accurately portrays Hess's bizarre behavior during the trial. He had been held in Britain since his 1941 solo flight there and claimed throughout the trial to have amnesia — then dramatically announced in court that he had been faking it. He was sentenced to life imprisonment and served the remainder of his life alone in Spandau Prison, dying in 1987 at 93. All of this is depicted faithfully.

Albert Speer
The Nazi who said sorry — and the one most likely to make audiences uncomfortable about it
Historic

Speer's strategy of admitting guilt, distancing himself from knowledge of the Holocaust, and appealing to the tribunal's Western sensibilities is depicted accurately. He was the only defendant to do so and received 20 years instead of death. Historians have long debated whether his "I didn't know" defense was genuine — the film lets that ambiguity breathe rather than resolving it neatly, which is the right call.

Col. Burton Andrus
The commandant of the prison — a stickler running the strangest detention facility in history
Historic

Andrus was the real American colonel in charge of the defendants' custody at Nuremberg. His strict oversight of the prisoners — including the helmets worn during cell checks and rules against neckties that could be used for suicide — is all historically documented. His personal frustration at Göring's suicide on his watch was very real.

Joachim von Ribbentrop
Former Foreign Minister — broken, confused, and first to hang
Historic

The film accurately captures Ribbentrop's disintegration during the trial. He had been one of Hitler's most powerful officials but appeared visibly confused and mentally diminished in the dock. He was indeed the first to be executed, on October 16, 1946. His rambling final statement is faithfully rendered.

Dr. Gustav Gilbert
The prison psychologist — quietly gathering the most remarkable set of psychological profiles in history
Historic

Gilbert was the real American Army psychologist assigned to the defendants, and his access to them produced the book Nuremberg Diary (1947), a key historical source. His observations, including his measurements of each defendant's IQ, his famous test of showing defendants concentration camp footage, and his direct conversations with Göring, are depicted with remarkable accuracy in the film.

Story

Largely Historic
The formation of the International Military Tribunal
True

The London Charter establishing the IMT was signed by the four Allied powers (U.S., UK, France, USSR) on August 8, 1945. The film accurately depicts the jurisdictional debates and ideological tensions among the Allies — particularly the Soviet Union's insistence on including the Katyn massacre among the charges (which was quietly dropped when it became clear Germany hadn't done it). The four-power structure is accurately shown throughout.

The screening of concentration camp footage in the courtroom
True

The November 29, 1945 screening of Nazi documentary footage — including footage from liberated camps — is one of the most well-documented moments of the trial. The film's depiction of defendants watching the footage and reacting with visible horror, denial, and shame is supported by court records and Gilbert's diary. Göring's reported comment that the footage was "perhaps a mistake" given international opinion is historically sourced.

Göring's cross-examination of Jackson
True

Jackson's cross-examination of Göring is accurately depicted as a disaster for the prosecution. Göring was well-prepared, articulate, and dominated the exchanges for days. The tribunal eventually intervened to require shorter answers. Jackson himself later admitted in letters that Göring had bested him. This is one of the most candid portrayals of prosecutorial failure in any courtroom drama.

The individual defenses of the accused
Largely Historic

The film compresses 11 months of proceedings into roughly 3 hours, so individual defendants receive varying amounts of screen time. The defenses depicted — Speer's distancing strategy, Hess's amnesia theatrics, Keitel and Jodl claiming soldier's duty, Kaltenbrunner denying knowledge of exterminations — all accurately reflect the actual defense strategies employed. Some defendants receive little or no screen time, which is a compression choice rather than a distortion.

The verdicts and executions
True

The verdicts are accurately rendered: 12 death sentences (Göring, Ribbentrop, Keitel, Kaltenbrunner, Rosenberg, Frank, Frick, Streicher, Sauckel, Jodl, Seyss-Inquart, and Bormann in absentia), 3 life sentences (Hess, Funk, Raeder), 4 prison terms (Speer 20 years, von Schirach 20 years, Neurath 15 years, Dönitz 10 years), and 3 acquittals (Schacht, von Papen, Fritzsche). Göring's cyanide suicide the night before his execution is faithfully depicted.

Jackson's personal and romantic subplot
Partly Historic

The film depicts a romantic involvement between Jackson and his secretary, Elsie Douglas. Douglas was a real person and was genuinely close to Jackson throughout the trials — she was one of his most trusted staff members. However, the nature of their relationship has been embellished for dramatic purposes. The film uses this as a humanizing device for Jackson, and while their closeness is documented, the romantic framing goes beyond what historical sources confirm.

Setting

Historic
The Palace of Justice, Nuremberg
Historic

The choice of Nuremberg itself was deliberate — it was the symbolic home of the Nazi Party rallies and one of the few German cities with a courthouse and prison complex still largely intact after Allied bombing. The film accurately depicts Courtroom 600, the adjacent prison wing, and the physical setup of the dock, press gallery, and translation booths. The simultaneous four-language translation system used at Nuremberg was genuinely one of the first of its kind in legal history.

The defendants' dock and prison conditions
Historic

The film accurately depicts the famous wooden dock where defendants sat in two rows, the guard standing behind each defendant, and the strict security protocols including the removal of ties and belts. The prison cells, psychiatric examination sessions, and the outdoor exercise area are depicted in line with documented accounts by Gilbert, Andrus, and journalists who covered the trials.

Postwar Nuremberg and occupied Germany
Largely Historic

The film portrays a bombed-out, bleak Nuremberg under Allied occupation — which is accurate. By 1945, roughly 90% of Nuremberg's city center had been destroyed by Allied bombing. The depictions of occupation-era social dynamics, the press corps, and the Allied officers' social world in Nuremberg are largely accurate in tone if not in every specific detail. Production design was clearly well-researched.

Fun Facts

The real Courtroom 600 still exists. The Palace of Justice in Nuremberg is still a functioning courthouse today. Courtroom 600 is open as a memorial and museum. You can sit in the actual room where Göring and the others were tried.
Göring's cyanide capsule is still a mystery. To this day, no one knows how Göring obtained the cyanide. It was found hidden in a brass cartridge case. Theories range from a sympathetic guard to a hidden compartment in a jar of skin cream he was allowed. Colonel Andrus was reportedly livid.
The simultaneous translation was invented for this trial. IBM developed the four-channel simultaneous interpretation system specifically for Nuremberg. Each participant wore headphones and could dial between English, French, Russian, and German. It was the birth of modern conference interpretation.
Göring's IQ was measured at 138. Dr. Gilbert administered IQ tests to all the defendants. The average among them was 128. Schacht scored highest at 143. Göring came in second at 138. Streicher (the virulent antisemitic publisher) came in lowest at 106. Gilbert published all of this in Nuremberg Diary.
The ashes of the executed were dumped in a river — secretly. To prevent any grave from becoming a Nazi shrine, the bodies of the ten executed defendants were cremated and their ashes were scattered into the Isar River at a classified location. The location remained secret for decades.
The film was nearly a theatrical movie. Nuremberg was originally developed as a feature film but was reshaped into a TNT miniseries to allow more time to cover the scope of the actual trials. It aired in July 2000 to significant ratings and was well-received by historians for its fidelity to the source material.

// Why It Matters

The Nuremberg trials established the legal concept that individuals — including heads of state — can be held personally responsible for war crimes and crimes against humanity. Robert Jackson's opening statement is still taught in international law courses. This is the trial that created the framework for every international criminal tribunal since.
